## What We Learned from Case 1

### What Worked Well
‚úÖ **Student engagement**: Students took the board simulation seriously and engaged deeply  
‚úÖ **Trade-off analysis**: Students understood there were no perfect solutions  
‚úÖ **Peer influence**: Students' arguments during deliberation actually changed votes  
‚úÖ **AI tool usage**: Students used AI appropriately to research and analyze options  
‚úÖ **Realistic scenarios**: Business context felt authentic and relevant

### Gaps Observed
‚ùå **Baseline comparison**: Students didn't consider that human loan officers were never audited for bias - they assumed human process was fair and only questioned the AI  
‚ùå **Second-order effects**: Some students focused on immediate decision without thinking about longer-term implications  
‚ùå **Stakeholder depth**: Students identified stakeholders but didn't always analyze impacts deeply

### Adjustments for Future Cases
- Make baseline comparisons more explicit in scenarios
- Encourage students to question ALL systems (human or AI)
- Push for deeper stakeholder impact analysis
- Continue emphasizing that reasonable people can disagree

## Master Rubrics Overview

The course uses six comprehensive rubrics (see MASTER-RUBRICS.md for full details):

### 1. Prompt Quality Rubric ‚≠ê PRIMARY FOR CARD GAME
**Evaluates** (4 dimensions):
- Specificity & Context: Does prompt provide clear business context?
- Strategic Value: Does it ask AI to help think, not just give answers?
- Critical Evaluation: Does it set up follow-up evaluation of AI's response?
- Iterative Thinking: Does it show plan for refining based on AI response?

**Scoring**:
- Exceptional (95-100%): Sophisticated prompts that uncover blind spots
- Excellent (90-94%): Strong strategic prompts with clear follow-up
- Proficient (80-89%): Solid prompts that get useful information
- Developing (70-79%): Basic prompts that lack specificity
- Needs Improvement (60-69%): Asks AI to make decisions
- Insufficient (Below 60%): Unclear or inappropriate prompts

### 2. Written Analysis Rubric
Evaluates quality of written responses to case questions

### 3. AI Collaboration Rubric
Assesses effective partnership with AI tools

### 4. Deliberation Participation Rubric
Used for board meeting participation and teamwork

### 5. Business Acumen Rubric
Evaluates practical business thinking and judgment

### 6. Ethical Awareness Rubric
Assesses consideration of stakeholder impacts as business factors

## Card Game Context & Goals

### Why a Card Game for Sessions 3-4?

**Strategic Position in Course**:
- **After foundations** (Sessions 1-2): Students have basic AI literacy
- **Before case studies** (Sessions 5-8): Need to practice prompt skills in real-time
- **Mid-semester energy**: Games are engaging when students might be hitting fatigue

**What Card Game Provides That Case Studies Don't**:
1. **Real-time pressure**: No time to overthink, must collaborate with AI quickly
2. **Multiple attempts**: See 17 different prompt approaches to same challenges
3. **Strategic gameplay**: Risk/reward decisions (accept challenge or pass)
4. **Immediate feedback**: See what works and doesn't work in real-time
5. **Peer learning**: Learn from watching others' successes and struggles
6. **Lower stakes**: Game format feels less intimidating than formal case analysis
7. **Competition**: Creates energy and engagement
8. **Memorable**: Students will remember the game experience

**Skills Bridge to Case Studies**:
- Prompt quality ‚Üí Will need for Case 1 & 2 pre-meeting assignments
- AI consultation ‚Üí Will use AI during case study deliberations  
- Debate skills ‚Üí Will present and defend positions in board meetings
- Strategic thinking ‚Üí Will evaluate complex business decisions
- Time pressure ‚Üí Case deliberations have time limits too

### Target Learning Outcomes for Card Game

By end of Sessions 3-4, students should be able to:
- ‚úÖ Write prompts that provide sufficient context and business framing
- ‚úÖ Ask AI strategic questions (not just ask for answers)
- ‚úÖ Iterate on AI responses with follow-up questions
- ‚úÖ Debate AI ethics topics with evidence and logic
- ‚úÖ Make strategic risk/reward decisions under pressure
- ‚úÖ Learn from observing peers' different approaches
- ‚úÖ Have fun while developing practical AI collaboration skills

## Design Constraints & Considerations

### Time Constraints
- **2 hours 15 minutes** per session (Sessions 3 & 4)
- Need time for: setup, warm-up, main game, debrief, reflection
- Must include consultation breaks where teams can use AI tools
- Cannot go over time (institutional scheduling is strict)

### Technology Available
- **Classroom setup**: Standard classroom with projector or large screen
- **Student devices**: Students bring laptops or tablets (BYOD)
- **Internet access**: Reliable WiFi for AI tool access
- **AI tools available**: ChatGPT, Claude, and others (students' choice)
- **No special software**: Must work with standard tools (Google Sheets, Word, etc.)

### Grading Requirements
- **Must tie to Master Rubrics**: Use existing rubric structure
- **50% of Sessions 3-4 grade**: Game performance (team + individual)
- **50% of Sessions 3-4 grade**: Post-game reflection assignment
- **Must be fair**: All students can succeed regardless of baseline skill
- **Must differentiate**: Can't give everyone same grade
- **Quick to score**: Instructor needs to grade efficiently

### Student Population Considerations
- **Mixed skill levels**: Some tech-savvy, some complete beginners
- **English language learners**: ~15% of class (need clear instructions)
- **Confidence issues**: Some students anxious about competition
- **Team dynamics**: Need strategic team assignment to balance skills
- **Participation anxiety**: Quieter students need safe ways to contribute

### Pedagogical Requirements
- **Active learning**: No passive lectures or reading
- **Inclusive design**: Everyone participates, not just confident students
- **Real-world application**: Skills must transfer to actual work
- **Ethical grounding**: Maintain focus on responsible AI use
- **No right answers**: Avoid making it feel like trivia or quiz

- **Student-centered**: Focus on what helps students learn, not what's easiest to grade
- **Practical over theoretical**: Would rather students can DO things than recite concepts
- **Authentic scenarios**: Use real business situations, not academic exercises

## Important Notes for Game Design

### Maintain Consistency with Course
- Use same vocabulary and frameworks students already know
- Reference Master Rubrics explicitly (students have seen these)
- Scenarios should feel similar to Case 1 (business context, ethical tensions)
- Keep "no right answer" philosophy central

### Respect Student Demographics
- Scenarios accessible to non-experts (no deep technical knowledge required)
- Business contexts span multiple industries (not just tech companies)
- Include small/medium business scenarios (not just Fortune 500)
- Consider that some students are English language learners

### Enable Instructor Success
- Mike will be running this solo (no teaching assistant)
- Scoring must be fast enough to keep game moving
- Instructions must be crystal clear (students won't have time to re-read)
- Materials should be ready to use with minimal prep

### Transfer to Future Semesters
- Design should work for 12-20 students (class size varies)
- Cards should remain relevant even as AI tools evolve
- Game mechanics should be simple enough to explain to new students
- Materials should be easy for Mike to update/modify
